Foundation

Mission, Vision
& Goal

Before we talk about what Velox does, let's be honest about the problem it exists to solve.

Somewhere in your waiting room right now — or in a stack of incoming referrals, or in a chart that hasn't been reviewed yet — there is a patient who needed to be seen sooner. Not because anyone made a mistake. Not because the care wasn't competent. But because the system that connects patients to the right care at the right time was never designed to be fast or precise. It was designed to be manageable.

Patients are referred with incomplete workups. Support staff triage by instinct. Specialists see patients who didn't need a specialist. PCPs refer patients who could have been managed in primary care — sometimes because they weren't sure, sometimes because they didn't have the latest guideline top of mind, and sometimes because it was easier than the alternative. High-risk patients wait in the same queue as low-risk ones. Time to treatment stretches from weeks to months.

Velox is built on a simple premise: the information needed to make better decisions already exists in the medical record. The latest guidelines already define who needs urgent attention and who doesn't. The gap is not clinical knowledge — it's the time and tools to apply that knowledge to every patient, every time.

Velox doesn't tell clinicians what to think. It gives them what they need to ask the right question before the patient walks in the door — or before they walk out without the care they needed.

Mission

Reduce time to treatment by giving specialist support staff and primary care providers deterministic, guideline-driven clinical decision support — so every referred patient is triaged at the right priority, and every patient encounter benefits from the latest standards of care.

Vision

A clinical world where the pathway from referral to treatment is smooth, transparent, and evidence-aligned — where no high-risk patient waits in a routine queue, no PCP misses a screening that guidelines say they should be doing, and no specialist sees a patient who wasn't ready to be seen.

Goal

Begin with specialist practices in Louisiana — building an alpha program with physicians who are willing to validate the tool against their own patients and their own clinical judgment. Then expand to primary care, extending guideline intelligence to the point where most clinical decisions are actually made: in the PCP's office, before the referral is ever written.

Overview

Four Moments,
One Tool

Velox fits into four distinct points in the care journey. Each one solves a different problem for a different person.

The four use cases aren't separate products. They're the same underlying system — reading the same EHR data, applying the same guidelines — answering different questions depending on who is asking and when.

Chapter 01 · Specialist MA
The Incoming Referral
A new referral arrives. Before anyone picks up the phone, Velox has already scored the patient against the relevant guideline, flagged what's missing, and set a scheduling priority. Your MA knows what to do.
Chapter 02 · PCP Pre-Referral
Before the Referral Is Written
The PCP is about to send a patient to a specialist. Velox shows them what the specialist will need at the first visit — and whether the patient is ready to go yet.
Chapter 03 · PCP Mid-Visit
The Patient Is in the Room
During the visit, the PCP notices something worth investigating. Velox surfaces guideline-driven risk patterns hiding in the existing chart data — conditions the patient may not have been evaluated for yet.
Chapter 04 · PCP Managing
No Referral Needed
The patient scores low risk. The guideline endorses primary care management. Velox generates a specific plan — what to do today, what to monitor, and exactly when a referral would become necessary.

Two things run through all four use cases. First, every recommendation is tied to a specific guideline with a specific citation — GOLD 2026 for COPD, AGA 2026 for MASLD, ESC/ERS 2022 for pulmonary hypertension. Velox doesn't express opinions. It applies the guidelines that the relevant specialty societies have already published. Second, every recommendation can be adjusted by your practice. If your thresholds differ from the guideline defaults — and there are good clinical reasons they sometimes should — those preferences are configurable, documented, and yours.

Chapter 01 · Specialist MA

The Specialist's
Front Door

Before the specialist sees a single chart, Velox has already read it — and knows what it means.

The problem this solves

When a referral arrives at a specialist practice, someone on the support staff has to decide how urgently to schedule it. That person is typically a medical assistant or care coordinator. They're competent, experienced, and working with what they have — which is usually a referral note, whatever labs made it into the record, and their clinical intuition.

That's not enough. Not because they're not good at their jobs, but because no human being should have to know that a COPD patient with two moderate exacerbations in the past year is categorically different from a COPD patient with one severe exacerbation — and that the GOLD 2026 guidelines classify both as Group E, urgently requiring specialist attention, while a COPD patient with zero exacerbations and a CAT score under 10 is Group A and can wait. That's not clinical intuition. That's guideline literacy that takes years to develop and minutes to apply with the right tool.

What Velox does for the specialist practice is simple: it reads the referral, reads the chart, scores the patient against the current guideline for their condition, and tells the MA what they're looking at — in plain language, with a clear priority, and with a specific list of what's missing before the specialist can give the patient their best first visit.

What the MA sees

When a referral comes in, Velox produces three things before anyone has to ask for them. First, a scoring trace — a step-by-step walkthrough of how the patient scored against the guideline, written so that someone without clinical training can follow the reasoning. Not just "high risk" but why: which labs, which history, which thresholds were crossed. Second, a scheduling priority: Routine, Standard, Priority, or Critical. Third, a list of what's missing — the specific labs or measurements that aren't in the record yet but are required for a complete assessment. Those become orders before the patient arrives.

What the specialist gains

The specialist's first visit with a referred patient is currently a coin flip. Sometimes the workup is complete and they can act. More often, they spend the first visit figuring out what they need to order before the second visit can be productive. Velox eliminates that problem. By the time the patient walks in, the FIB-4 has been calculated, the spirometry has been done, the missing labs have been collected. The first visit becomes a real clinical encounter instead of a data-gathering exercise.

Your MA doesn't need to know the GOLD 2026 exacerbation classification system. She needs to know that this patient should be called today, not scheduled in six weeks. Velox tells her that. The guideline explains why.

Chapter 02 · PCP Pre-Referral

Before the Referral
Is Written

The best time to complete a workup isn't after the specialist asks for it. It's before the patient leaves the PCP's office.

The problem this solves

When a PCP refers a patient to a specialist, they're making a judgment call: this patient needs more than I can give them. That call is usually right. What's often incomplete is the preparation that should accompany it. The specialist receives the referral, reviews the chart, and realizes that the FIB-4 was never calculated, or the spirometry is three years old, or the echocardiogram was done at another facility and never made it into the record. The first specialist visit becomes a triage visit — figure out what's needed, order it, and schedule a real assessment in six more weeks.

Velox gives the PCP a pre-referral checklist that isn't a generic form — it's the specific workup the target specialist will need, derived from the guideline for that condition, applied to this specific patient's existing chart data. If the FIB-4 can already be calculated from labs that are already in the record, Velox calculates it and shows the PCP where their patient stands before the referral is written. If it can't be calculated because a lab is missing, the PCP knows what to order before the patient leaves today.

Two ways to use it

PCPs will use this tool in two different modes, and both matter. In the first mode, the PCP knows which specialty they're referring to. Their patient has MASLD features and they're sending her to hepatology. Velox evaluates the patient against the MASLD pathway specifically and shows exactly what the hepatologist will need. Targeted, specific, actionable.

In the second mode, the presentation is non-specific. The patient has persistent abdominal discomfort, a history of T2DM, mildly elevated liver enzymes, and worsening fatigue. The PCP isn't sure whether this is a GI referral, an endocrinology question, or something else. Velox evaluates the patient across all relevant pathways and surfaces what each one would need — letting the PCP and the data together guide the referral decision.

What changes for the specialist

When Velox is used in pre-referral mode, the specialist receives a different kind of referral. Not just "MASLD evaluation requested" but a patient who has already had their FIB-4 calculated, already had a FibroScan ordered and resulted, and arrives with a complete AGA 2026 workup ready for interpretation. The first visit becomes what it should be: a clinical encounter where decisions are made, not a data collection exercise where orders are placed.

The specialist who helped define the referral criteria is now receiving referrals that meet those criteria. That changes the entire character of the relationship between primary care and specialty.

For the specialist

Chapter 03 · PCP Mid-Visit

The Mid-Visit
Moment

The patient is in the room. The chart has information in it that no one has connected yet. Velox connects it.

The problem this solves

A PCP sees hundreds of patients. They cannot maintain encyclopedic recall of every guideline update for every condition they might encounter on any given day. They're also seeing patients for one reason — a diabetes follow-up, a hypertension check, a well visit — while that same patient's chart contains signals for a condition that hasn't been discussed yet. Nobody is at fault. The information is there. The connection just hasn't been made.

The mid-visit use case is about surfacing those connections in real time. Not after the patient leaves. Not on a quality improvement report next quarter. Now, while the patient is still in the room and something can actually be done about it.

Importantly, this use case does not require a diagnosis. It doesn't ask the PCP to enter ICD codes or make a clinical assessment before it can help. It reads what's already in the chart — labs, vitals, medications, problem list, demographics — and identifies whether any guideline-defined risk pattern is present that the PCP should know about. The physician still makes every clinical decision. Velox just makes sure they have the relevant information before the visit ends.

What this looks like

The PCP opens Velox during or just before the visit and runs a broad scan. In seconds, the tool surfaces any pathway where the patient's existing data matches guideline-defined risk criteria — not because they have a diagnosis, but because the objective numbers in their chart meet a threshold that the guideline says warrants evaluation. Each finding comes with the specific data that triggered it, the guideline that defines the threshold, and a clear statement of what the next step would be.

This isn't a list of everything that could theoretically be wrong. It's a short, specific, justified list of things the patient's own data suggests should be looked at — with citations, not opinions.

The PCP doesn't need to remember that T2DM patients should be screened for MASLD. Velox remembers it, checks whether the FIB-4 has ever been done, and says something if it hasn't. That's the entire mechanism.

Value proposition

For the PCP
Guideline recall without the cognitive load
Stop maintaining mental checklists across dozens of guideline updates. Velox does it. You make the clinical decisions. The guidelines just stay current automatically.
For the patient
Screening that happens at the right visit
Not next year. Not when someone finally connects the dots. Today, while the patient is already there, already engaged, and the PCP has a few minutes to address something new.
For the specialist
Earlier, better-prepared referrals
When the PCP catches something early, the specialist sees it earlier. Earlier disease. More options. Better outcomes.
For the system
Guidelines applied to real patients, reliably
The gap between what guidelines recommend and what happens in practice narrows. Standards of care become something that actually happens, not aspirational targets.

Chapter 04 · PCP Managing

Keeping the Right
Patients in
Primary Care

A referral is not always the answer. Sometimes the guideline says the PCP is exactly the right person for this patient — and gives them a specific plan to prove it.

The problem this solves

Not every patient who gets referred to a specialist needs to be there. This isn't a criticism — it's a systems problem. PCPs refer partly because they're uncertain, and uncertainty is reasonable when guidelines are changing and the workup is incomplete. They also refer because referring is safer than not referring, because patients sometimes expect it, and because the PCP doesn't always have a clear picture of what "managing in primary care" actually looks like for a specific condition at a specific risk level.

The result is specialist schedules full of low-risk patients who could have been managed well in primary care — while high-risk patients wait for slots that should have been available. Every unnecessary referral delays a necessary one.

This use case is about giving PCPs something they currently don't have: a guideline-endorsed, specific, actionable management plan for low-risk patients — complete with what to do today, what to prescribe, what to monitor, how often to recheck, and exactly what would change the answer from "manage in primary care" to "refer now." That last piece is critical. A PCP who knows the precise threshold that triggers a referral is practicing with confidence, not anxiety.

What the management plan contains

When Velox scores a patient as low risk and the relevant guideline endorses primary care management, the output isn't just a verdict — it's a complete handoff. The PCP sees: a plain-language summary of why this patient doesn't need a specialist today, the specific lifestyle and pharmacologic interventions the guideline recommends for this risk level, a monitoring plan with a specific reassessment timeline, the exact labs to order at the next visit, and the precise thresholds — in numbers, not generalities — that would trigger a referral on reassessment.

None of this is invented. Every element is drawn from the guideline with a citation attached. The PCP isn't being told what to do by a software tool. They're being given a structured view of what the guideline already says, applied to this specific patient.

What this means for the specialist

This is where the tension in the design requires honest discussion. A tool that keeps patients in primary care is, on its face, a tool that reduces specialist volume. That's only a problem if the patients being kept in primary care are ones the specialist should be seeing. They're not. The patients managed in primary care under this model are low risk by guideline definition — patients the specialist would see briefly, confirm the PCP was managing appropriately, and discharge back to primary care anyway. The specialist's schedule fills instead with the high-risk patients who actually need them.

More importantly: the specialist can configure exactly when they want to see a patient, regardless of guideline routing. If a pulmonologist believes every new COPD diagnosis should have at least one specialist visit — even Group A — that preference can be set in Velox. The guidelines define the default. The specialist defines the exceptions. Both are documented and transparent.

The goal isn't to keep patients away from specialists. It's to make sure that when a patient needs a specialist, they can get one — because the schedule isn't full of patients who didn't.

A note on what Velox is not

Velox does not make clinical decisions. It does not diagnose patients. It does not tell physicians what to prescribe. Every output is a structured presentation of what the relevant guideline says, applied to the specific data in the patient's chart, with the source cited. The clinician remains the clinician. The judgment remains theirs.

What Velox changes is the information available at the moment decisions are made. The MA who sees a Critical flag next to a COPD referral still decides to call the patient. The PCP who sees a FIB-4 of 2.87 and a suggestion to order a FibroScan still decides whether to order it. The specialist who receives a complete pre-referral workup still decides how to interpret it. Velox doesn't replace any of those decisions. It makes sure they happen with the right information, at the right time, with the right guideline in hand.

That's a narrow ambition. It's also, in our experience, the thing that matters most.
